The way Axiom products **sound** matters as much as how they look. Consistent, human, and helpful copy turns a confusing screen into an obvious next step. This page sets out the voice, tone, and writing rules every Axiom surface should follow.

## Voice vs. tone

- **Voice** is *who* we are. It never changes: confident, clear, and human.
- **Tone** is *how we adapt that voice* depending on the situation. The tone in an error message is calmer than the tone in a feature announcement.

Think of voice as your personality and tone as your mood.

## Our voice

Axiom products are used by procurement specialists, pricing analysts, sales teams, and developers — often under time pressure. Our voice is:

| We are | We are not |
|---|---|
| Clear | Curt |
| Confident | Boastful |
| Helpful | Patronising |
| Direct | Cold |
| Human | Casual |

### Concretely, that means

- Use plain English. Prefer "send" to "transmit", "fix" to "rectify".
- Write in the **active voice**: "We saved your changes" beats "Your changes have been saved".
- Address the user as **"you"** and the product as **"Axiom"** — never "we" mid-sentence unless representing the team.
- Cut filler. "Please note that you must…" → "You must…".
- Use contractions ("you're", "we'll") to sound natural. Avoid them in legal or compliance copy.

## Tone, by situation

| Situation | Tone | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Onboarding / empty state | Welcoming, instructive | "Build your first dashboard to see live KPIs from your data." |
| Success | Brief, positive | "Invoice sent." |
| Confirmation / destructive | Direct, factual | "Delete this contract? You can't undo this." |
| Error | Calm, helpful | "We couldn't connect to the server. Check your network and try again." |
| Loading | Reassuring | "Indexing 12,432 records…" |
| Marketing / release notes | Energetic, but never hype-y | "Faster bulk imports — now 3× quicker on large CSVs." |

Never use sarcasm, apologies for trivial things, or exclamation marks outside of celebratory success states.

## Writing rules

### Capitalisation

- **Sentence case** for everything: page titles, buttons, menu items, labels.
- Exceptions: proper nouns and product names ("Axiom").
- Don't capitalise common nouns ("dashboard", not "Dashboard").

### Punctuation

- End full sentences with a full stop, including in tooltips and helper text.
- No full stop on **labels**, **buttons**, **menu items**, or single-line strings.
- Use the Oxford comma.
- One space after a full stop.

### Numbers and dates

- Use numerals for everything ("3 items", not "three items"), except at the start of a sentence.
- Use the ISO short form where space is tight (`2026-05-27`); use the long form where there's room (`27 May 2026`).
- Localise units. Don't assume USD, °F, or imperial.

### Buttons and actions

- Start with a **verb**: "Save", "Create project", "Send invoice".
- Be specific about the outcome: "Delete contract" beats "OK".
- Cancel and dismiss should always be labelled "Cancel" — never "No" or "Back" when the action is "abandon this change".

## Error messages

A good error message tells the user three things:

1. **What happened** ("We couldn't save your draft.")
2. **Why** ("The server didn't respond.")
3. **What to do next** ("Wait a moment and try again, or contact support if this keeps happening.")

Don't:

- Don't blame the user ("You entered an invalid value").
- Don't expose internal error codes or stack traces in user-facing copy.
- Don't apologise ("Sorry!") more than once per error.

Do:

- Be specific about the field or action that failed.
- Suggest a next step the user can actually take.
- Use the same wording for the same error across the product.

## Microcopy patterns

| Pattern | Pattern wording | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Required field | Asterisk + footnote | "Fields marked * are required" |
| Optional field | Word "(optional)" after label | "Notes (optional)" |
| Empty state | Noun-first title | "No invoices" |
| Confirmation | Verb-first question | "Delete this contract?" |
| Loading | Verb in present participle + object | "Indexing records…" |
| Success | Past tense, no exclamation | "Invoice sent" |

## Inclusive language

- Use gender-neutral language by default. Prefer "they / them", "team members", "users".
- Avoid idioms and metaphors that don't translate well across cultures or languages.
- Don't use ableist phrases ("crazy", "insane", "dumb"). Choose plain alternatives.
- Avoid violent or war metaphors ("kill the process", "deploy attack").

## Don'ts

- Don't pile adjectives onto headings: "Powerful new advanced reporting features" → "New reporting features".
- Don't use jargon without defining it the first time.
- Don't write emojis into product copy. They survive better in release notes and changelogs.
- Don't tell the user something is "easy" or "simple" — let them decide.
